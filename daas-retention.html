Case study

Resubscribing to a DaaS Product

Using user behavior, product telemetry, and search analytics to identify adoption friction in a technical data platform and connect product improvements to subscription retention.

RoleProduct Manager for North and South America
ProductB2B2C research data subscription platform
UsersResearchers, chemistry librarians, institutional buyers
GoalIncrease utilization and reduce churn risk

Problem

Subscribed researchers were not consistently engaging with a curated data platform for materials science, physics, chemistry, and engineering. The business risk was clear: if end users did not use the product enough, institutional buyers had less justification to renew.

The specific product problem was that users performing material selection searches received results that did not match their expectations. This reduced click-through, shortened sessions, and created a direct path from poor search relevance to renewal risk.

My Role

As the Product Manager for North and South America, I owned the discovery work to understand why subscribed users were not fully engaging. I worked across customer insight, telemetry, stakeholder needs, and product strategy to define a focused problem before moving into solutioning.

Users and Stakeholders

The product served a B2B2C environment where the economic buyer and end user were different. Chemistry librarians and institutional buyers made renewal decisions, while researchers used the platform for technical workflows. The product had to create value for both groups: researcher usefulness and buyer-visible engagement.

Discovery

I used a mixed-methods approach combining online user observation, semi-quantitative questions, qualitative interviews, product telemetry, and search analytics. During recorded sessions, I allowed users to navigate naturally instead of steering them toward ideal workflows.

The recurring insight was that users were searching by broader material classes. They expected the product to understand grouped material categories, but the search experience was not consistently aligned with that mental model.

Data value framework used to connect behavioral insight to customer growth and utilization.

Decision and Tradeoffs

The team could have tried to improve every search path at once. Instead, we prioritized material class search because it matched the clearest observed user behavior and aligned with a planned semiconductor data module. Semiconductor materials were also among the most searched classes, making them a practical first wedge.

Solution

We proposed an additional search method that allowed users to search by material class. Existing metadata already captured many material classes and was ingested into the knowledge model. To address gaps, an SME enriched missing material class metadata, starting with semiconductor materials.

Impact

The product direction tied a user-facing improvement to a business-critical retention problem. Better search relevance was expected to increase engagement depth, improve confidence in the platform, and support annual subscriber retention by keeping usage above renewal thresholds.

Metrics

  • Increased search volume as a signal of greater interaction with the search experience.
  • Higher click-through rate from the results page, indicating improved relevance.
  • Longer session duration, showing deeper engagement with relevant technical content.
  • More file downloads, including Excel and CIF exports, reflecting higher user investment.
  • Higher annual subscriber retention, connecting product usage to buyer renewal confidence.

Reflection

This case shows how I approach technical product work: first understand the user’s mental model, then use data to clarify the business impact, and only then define a solution that is specific enough to ship.
